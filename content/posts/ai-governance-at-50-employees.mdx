Every few months a new enterprise AI governance framework drops — a 40-page document designed for organisations with dedicated ethics teams, legal counsel, and a risk committee. It's largely useless for a 50-person startup.

But "we don't need governance, we're a startup" is how you end up with customer data in a prompt you didn't mean to expose, or a model making decisions in a regulated domain without a human in the loop.

Here's what actually works at startup scale.

---

## The three decisions you need to make

Good AI governance at 50 employees isn't a framework. It's three decisions, clearly made, written down, and actually followed.

**Decision 1: What data can go into a prompt?**

This is the most urgent one. The moment you start using LLMs, someone will put customer data into a prompt and send it to an API operated by a third party. Maybe that's fine. Maybe it violates your privacy policy or your enterprise customer contracts.

Make the decision now: what categories of data are in-bounds for AI processing, and which aren't? Write it down. Tell your engineers. Revisit it quarterly.

**Decision 2: What decisions can AI make autonomously, and which need a human?**

Not all AI outputs are equal. A model that drafts a reply for a human to review is very different from a model that sends the reply. A model that suggests a credit limit is very different from a model that sets one.

Draw the line: here is where AI assists, and here is where AI decides. The line will move as you gain confidence in specific systems — but it should be explicit, not implicit.

**Decision 3: Who is accountable when something goes wrong?**

This is the one nobody wants to answer. If the model makes a bad call — a wrong customer communication, a biased output, a privacy exposure — who owns it? Product? Engineering? The team lead whose workflow it was integrated into?

Assign accountability before something goes wrong, not after.

---

## The lightweight infrastructure

Beyond the three decisions, you need three pieces of lightweight infrastructure:

- A **shared doc** where all active AI use cases are listed with their data usage and accountability owner. One page. Updated when things change.
- A **slack channel** (or equivalent) where engineers can flag AI-related decisions or concerns before shipping. Low friction, high visibility.
- A **quarterly 30-minute review** where the three decisions get revisited in light of how the systems are actually being used.

That's it. That's your AI governance for a 50-person company.

As you scale, the framework scales too — but you'll be scaling from a foundation that works, rather than retroactively imposing structure on a mess.
