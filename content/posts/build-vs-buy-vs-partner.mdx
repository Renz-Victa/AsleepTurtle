The build vs buy decision is one of the oldest questions in software. AI makes it harder, because there's now a third option — partner — and because the landscape changes fast enough that the right answer in January might be wrong in June.

Here's the framework we use with clients at the Series B and C stage.

---

## Start with the question nobody asks first

Before build vs buy, ask: *Does this AI capability create defensible competitive advantage, or does it enable a workflow?*

This is the most important question and the one most teams skip because they're excited about the technology rather than clear about the strategy.

If the answer is **workflow** — this AI capability is table stakes for operating at your scale, not a differentiator — then buy or partner, aggressively. You want to get to "done" as fast and cheaply as possible so your engineering team can work on the things that actually differentiate you.

If the answer is **competitive advantage** — your ability to do this better than anyone else is core to why customers choose you — then build. Own the data, own the model, own the feedback loop.

Most AI use cases at the Series B stage are workflows. Teams overestimate how many are competitive advantages because the technology is exciting.

---

## The decision matrix

For use cases where the answer isn't immediately clear, run through this matrix:

| Question | Build signal | Buy/Partner signal |
|---|---|---|
| Is this core to your differentiation? | Yes | No |
| Do you have proprietary data no vendor can access? | Yes | No |
| Do you have the team to build and maintain it? | Yes | No |
| Will you need to iterate on this continuously? | Yes | Rarely |
| Is a vendor's version already 80%+ of what you need? | No | Yes |
| Is this a regulated use case with data residency requirements? | Often | Check carefully |

If you score mostly Build signals: build. If you score mostly Buy/Partner signals: buy or partner. If you're split: default to Buy/Partner and revisit in 12 months once you have more data on usage patterns.

## Build vs Buy: the hidden costs teams miss

**Hidden costs of building:**
- Model evaluation and testing infrastructure (often underestimated 3–5x)
- Ongoing prompt engineering as model behaviour drifts
- Retraining or fine-tuning as your data evolves
- Monitoring and alerting for model degradation
- Engineering context that gets locked up in one or two people

**Hidden costs of buying:**
- Vendor lock-in as the use case evolves beyond the product's intended scope
- Data portability — can you get your data out if you switch?
- Rate limits and latency at scale
- Cost escalation as you grow (most AI SaaS pricing scales aggressively with usage)
- The cost of *not* owning the feedback loop over time

Neither option is free. The question is which costs you're better positioned to absorb.

---

## When "Partner" is the right answer

Partner means working with an external team — an agency, a consultancy, or a specialist — to build something you'll own and operate yourself. It's the right answer when:

- You want to build, not buy, but don't have the internal capability yet
- You want to move faster than you could by hiring
- You want to learn by doing — building capability in your team alongside the external partner

The key to a successful partner engagement: make sure knowledge transfer is built into the contract, not treated as an optional add-on. The goal is that by the end of the engagement, your team could rebuild it without external help.

---

## A note on timing

The AI landscape moves fast enough that a "buy" decision made today might be wrong in 18 months — either because a better product exists, or because your use case has evolved beyond what the product handles.

Build decisions have the opposite problem: what takes six months to build today might be a $50/month API in a year.

Neither of these is an argument for paralysis. They're arguments for building in explicit review points — "we'll revisit this decision in 12 months" — rather than treating the initial decision as permanent.
