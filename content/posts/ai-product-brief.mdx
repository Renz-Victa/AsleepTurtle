Most AI product briefs are either a one-liner ("use AI to improve onboarding") or a 20-page document that takes three weeks to write and is ignored by the time engineering starts. Neither works.

Here's the format we've converged on after watching a lot of briefs succeed and fail.

---

## The five sections that actually matter

**1. The problem in one sentence**

Not the solution. The problem. *"Support agents spend 40% of their time writing responses to questions already answered in our docs."* That's a problem brief. *"We want an AI assistant for support"* is a solution brief masquerading as a problem statement.

The reason this matters: a good problem statement constrains the solution space appropriately. It tells engineering where the target is without telling them exactly how to hit it.

**2. Success criteria (specific and measurable)**

What does "this worked" look like? Be precise:

- "Reduces average handle time for tier-1 tickets by 30%"
- "Achieves >85% acceptance rate on drafted responses"
- "Processes a document in under 3 seconds at p95"

Also specify the *primary* metric. If you have five metrics and no hierarchy, you'll get endless debates about which one matters most. Pick one. List the others as secondary.

**3. Acceptable failure modes**

This is the most underrated section and the one almost nobody writes.

Every AI system will be wrong sometimes. The question is: which kinds of wrong are acceptable, and which are dealbreakers?

For a draft email assistant: a slightly awkward tone is acceptable; sending PII to the wrong customer is not. For a document classifier: miscategorising 5% of documents is fine; silently dropping documents is not.

Writing this down forces the conversation before engineering starts, not after launch when something breaks.

**4. What's in scope for v1 — and what isn't**

The explicit "not in scope" list is often more valuable than the scope list. It closes off the scope creep conversations before they start, and it signals to engineering that you've thought carefully about tradeoffs.

"v1 handles English-language tickets only. Multi-language support is not in scope and will be evaluated in Q3."

**5. The definition of done for the first milestone**

Not the full project — just the first concrete thing that means you're making real progress. "A prototype that correctly handles 10 handpicked tickets with the right suggested response" is a milestone. "The AI works" is not.

---

## A template to steal

```
Problem: [one sentence describing the workflow pain]

Success metric (primary): [specific, measurable]
Success metrics (secondary): [2–3 others]

Acceptable failure modes: [what wrong looks like when it's okay]
Unacceptable failure modes: [what wrong looks like when it's a blocker]

v1 scope: [what it does]
Out of scope: [what it doesn't do]

First milestone: [specific, achievable in 1–2 weeks]
Owner: [who]
```

Fill this in before any engineering conversation. It takes 45 minutes and saves weeks.
