Most startups start their AI journey in the wrong place. They identify a use case, spin up a proof of concept, and three months later wonder why adoption is zero. The problem usually isn't the model — it's everything around the model.

Before you write a line of AI-related code, answer these six questions honestly. They won't take long. The answers will save you months.

---

## 1. Do you have a data foundation worth building on?

This is the one everyone talks about and the one most teams are dishonest with themselves about. "We have lots of data" is not the same as "we have clean, labelled, accessible data in a format a model can use."

Ask yourself:

- Is the relevant data in one place, or scattered across five tools?
- Is it clean enough that a new engineer could make sense of it in a week?
- Do you have the right *volume* — not just any data, but data that reflects the specific task you want to automate or augment?

If the answer to any of these is "sort of," you need a data cleanup sprint before an AI sprint. This isn't a blocker — it's a sequencing decision.

---

## 2. Is there a human doing this task today — and doing it badly?

The best AI use cases are ones where a human is currently doing the job, you can measure how well they're doing it, and you have examples of both good and bad outputs. This gives you a target, a training signal, and a baseline.

Use cases without a human analogue are harder. You're inventing a new workflow, which means you're simultaneously solving an AI problem and a change management problem. Doable — just harder.

---

## 3. Can you define what "good" looks like before you start?

This is the most commonly skipped step and the one most responsible for failed AI projects.

Before you build anything, write down:

- The metric you're optimising for (precision, recall, latency, cost per output, user satisfaction — pick one primary)
- The minimum acceptable threshold ("if it gets this right 80% of the time, we'll ship it")
- The failure modes you're willing to tolerate vs. the ones that are dealbreakers

Without this, you'll spend weeks debating whether the model is "good enough" with no shared definition of good.

---

## 4. Does your team have the capability to maintain what you build?

The model is not the product. The product is the model *plus* the prompts, the evaluation framework, the monitoring, the feedback loop, and the retraining strategy. All of that needs ongoing care.

Ask honestly: if the consultant leaves, can your team keep this running and improving? If the answer is no, you have two options — build internal capability as part of the engagement, or scope the project more narrowly to what your team can own.

---

## 5. Is there organisational buy-in at the level that matters?

AI projects die in the gap between "the CTO thinks this is cool" and "the team whose workflow will change has no idea this is coming." Both are required: executive sponsorship and genuine buy-in from the people who will use (or be affected by) the output.

The latter is harder to get and more important to have. A model that a team ignores is a model that fails, regardless of its benchmark scores.

---

## 6. Is this the highest-leverage thing your engineering team could be working on?

This is the question most consultants won't ask you because the answer might be "no." But it's worth asking.

AI projects consume significant engineering time — not just to build, but to evaluate, iterate, and maintain. If there are faster, cheaper paths to the same outcome (better tooling, a workflow change, a third-party product), it's worth knowing before you commit.

The goal isn't to avoid AI — it's to use it where it genuinely creates leverage.

---

## Putting it together

Run through these six questions with your team before any scoping conversation. Score each one: green (confident yes), amber (partially there), red (not yet).

If you have more than two reds, pause and address the reds first. If you have mostly greens and ambers, you're ready to move.
